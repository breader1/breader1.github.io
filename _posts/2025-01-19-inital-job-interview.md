#### **Apply for Jobs as a New Grad**

It’s January 2025. The last three years of my life have been spent studying computer science at BCIT, a decision I made to shift my career—a daunting move, especially as someone who hadn’t been in school for over a decade. It was challenging and, at times, incredibly stressful. But through hard work and persistence, I graduated with distinction in December 2024.

Then reality set in. The "easy part" was over. Now came the daunting task of finding a job. Throughout the latter part of my education, I kept hearing how difficult it was for new grads to land their first role. It felt like I was about to face an impossible challenge.

#### **The Job Hunt Begins**

Since early January, I’ve been applying to every opportunity I could find. At the time of writing, I’ve already received rejections from four out of five applications. Although I’m still early in the process, it’s hard to block out the negative thoughts: *“It’s going to take six months to a year to even get a call,”* or, *“You need to be a senior developer before you can even land a junior role.”*

While I was in school, I was fortunate to be taught by amazing instructors. One of them referred me to a company looking for a junior full-stack developer, with a focus on backend work—right in my wheelhouse, given my academic projects and current personal work. After researching the company, I realized it would be a perfect fit for me as a new grad. I sent in my application, and to my surprise, I received a response the next day asking for a phone interview. I was ecstatic—and nervous. It had been a long time since I’d done an interview, and I worried I might be too rusty.

#### **The Interview**

The interview was scheduled for the following day. I spent the entire day before preparing: reviewing my past projects, brushing up on my current work, and revisiting questions I’d encountered in previous interviews. I wanted to be as prepared as possible.

The interview went well. I was able to confidently answer the questions, and I made sure to ask a few of my own to show genuine interest in the role. What was supposed to be a one-hour conversation stretched into nearly two. The interviewers were warm and welcoming, which helped calm my nerves. (If you’re reading this, thank you for that!) I went to bed that night feeling optimistic.

#### **The Day After**

Less than 12 hours later, I received a text and email inviting me to a second interview—on very short notice. I had only two hours to prepare. While this was far from ideal, I wasn’t about to let the tight timeline deter me. It felt like walking into a final exam I hadn’t studied for. The second interview was a technical coding challenge, and I worried I wasn’t ready. But I did what I could with the time I had and dove in headfirst, fully expecting to struggle.

#### **The Second Interview**

The second interview was conducted by a friendly and supportive group, which eased some of my nerves. They explained the process: I’d be given two technical coding problems and have a chance to ask questions about the company afterward. 

The first question was straightforward: *“Remove duplicates from an array.”* I was thrilled! I’d encountered this problem before and quickly implemented a solution using a `Set`. Confidence soared. 

But then came the second question: *“Group anagrams.”* The wind left my sails. I hadn’t seen this question before, and—to my embarrassment—I didn’t even know what an anagram was. I tried to piece together a brute-force solution to understand the problem better, knowing it wouldn’t be efficient. 

Thankfully, the interviewer was incredibly supportive and walked me through the optimal solution. Despite their help, my brain felt like it short-circuited. I fumbled basic syntax, forgot how to use a `for` loop with a dictionary, and struggled to articulate my thoughts. It was a humbling experience, to say the least.

That said, I’m grateful for the learning opportunity. Even though I struggled, I remained determined to solve the problem and demonstrate my willingness to learn—a personal goal I’d set for myself.

#### **The Aftermath**

When the coding portion ended, I felt defeated. I was embarrassed by my performance on the second question, especially after learning the optimal solution: sorting the anagrams and using them as dictionary keys to group words. I asked the interviewers a few questions about the company before the interview concluded, but I couldn’t shake the feeling that I’d blown my chance.

While I won’t know the outcome until Monday, I’m staying positive. Regardless of the result, this experience has been a valuable learning opportunity. I’ve gained insights I can carry into future interviews. At the end of the day, my goal is to grow a little as a developer every day. Over time, that growth will lead to success.

I’ll update this post with the outcome of the interview.
