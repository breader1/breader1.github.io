#### **Apply For Jobs As A New Grad**

It's currently January 2025. I've spent the last 3 years studying computer science at BCIT in order to shift my career (a very scary thing to do as someone who hasn't been in school for over 10 years). It was hard, and at times very stressful. But after fighting through the program, hard work paid off, and I graduated with distinction in December 2024. Then it set in—the "easy part" was over. Now I had to find a job. From what I'd been hearing during the latter part of my education, getting a job as a new grad seemed like an impossible task.

#### **The Job Hunt Begins**

Since early January, I've been applying to basically anything I could. At the time of writing this, I've already received rejections from 4 out of 5 employers. Even though it's still super early in the search, it's hard not to get sucked into negative thoughts like, "It's going to take six months to a year to even get a call," or, "You need to become a senior developer before you can get a call for a junior position."

While I was in school, I was fortunate to be taught by some amazing instructors, and one of them referred me to a company looking for a junior full-stack developer, with a focus on backend development. (This felt like it was right in my wheelhouse since I'd done many backend-focused projects in school and was still working on others.) I did some research into the company, and it seemed like a perfect fit for me as a new grad. So, I sent in my application. Within a day, I got a response asking for a phone interview. I was ecstatic but also very nervous. It had been a long time since I'd done an interview, and I was worried I might be too rusty to perform well.

#### **The Interview**

The interview was scheduled for the following day. I spent the entire day before preparing. I reviewed all the projects I'd completed in school and the ones I was currently working on. I also revisited questions I'd been asked in past interviews to make sure I was as prepared as possible.

The interview went well. I was able to answer all the questions they asked and also asked some of my own, which I think helped show my interest in the position. What was originally scheduled to be a one-hour interview turned into nearly two hours! The overall impression I got was amazing—the interviewers made me feel welcome and comfortable. (If you're reading this, thank you for that!) I went to bed that night feeling confident I had done well.

#### **The Day After**

Less than 12 hours later, I received a text message and an email inviting me to a second interview—with very short notice. I had only two hours to prepare, which was far less time than I would've liked, but I wasn't going to let that stop me. The situation felt a lot like walking into a final exam I hadn't studied for. I was nervous and got in my own head about it because the second interview was a technical coding interview. I hadn't practiced these kinds of questions since school, and I was extremely worried I might fall flat on my face. Despite my worries, I spent the two hours prepping as much as I could and dove in headfirst, fully expecting to crash and burn.

#### **The Second Interview**

The second interview started with a warm welcome from the interviewers, which helped ease my nerves. They explained how it would go: I’d be asked two technical questions, and then I’d have a chance to ask them questions after the coding portion was over.

The first question was simple: *“Remove duplicates from an array.”* “YES!” I thought. I’d seen this before and knew about using a `Set`, which doesn’t allow duplicates. I passed the input array to a `Set`, then returned the `Set` cast as an array. BOOM! 

I was feeling confident—until the second question hit: *“Group anagrams.”* My confidence evaporated. I’d never seen this question before and (you can laugh at this) didn’t even know what an anagram was. I was stumped. I decided to go with a brute-force solution to better understand the problem, knowing it would be inefficient (O(n³)) but hoping it might help me figure things out. 

I was extremely fortunate to have such a great interviewer. I let them know I was stuck on the optimal solution, and they walked me through how to get there. But during the walkthrough, my brain completely melted. I swear I forgot how to code. I couldn’t even make an array. I was stumbling over words, making simple syntax errors, and forgetting how to use a `for` loop on a dictionary. It was embarrassing to the max. Eventually, we got to the solution, but I felt like a complete idiot.

Despite all of that, I was happy we solved it at all. I learned a TON from the question and the small errors I was making. My biggest hope was that I showed, even though I struggled, I was eager to learn and kept trying to improve. Personally, my goal was to demonstrate my enthusiasm to learn.

#### **The Aftermath**

The coding portion was over, and I felt like an absolute BLOB. I was super embarrassed about fumbling the second question, especially after learning the optimal solution (sort the anagrams, then use them as dictionary keys, appending each word to a list in the dictionary). I asked the interviewers questions about the company, and then the interview ended. I was left feeling like I’d fumbled the ball and had no chance of getting the job. I was extremely disappointed in myself and was sure I’d blown my chance.

I won’t know the outcome until Monday, but I’m staying positive. I learned a lot through this process that I can take into future interviews. Regardless of the result, there’s been growth here, and all I’m trying to do is improve as a developer a little bit each day. Over time, that will lead to success.

I’ll update this post with the outcome of the interview.
