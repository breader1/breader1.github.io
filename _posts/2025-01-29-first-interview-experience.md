#### **Apply For Jobs As A New Grad**
It's currently Janurary 2025. I've spent the last 3 years studying computer science at BCIT in order to shift my career. (which was a very scary thing to do as person that hasn't been in school for over 10 years). It was hard, and at times very stressful. But after fighting through the program, hard work was rewarded, and I was able to graduate with distinction in December 2024. Then it set in, the "easy part" was over, now I had to find a job; From what I've been hearing in the latter part of my education, getting a job as a new grad was a seemingly impossible task.

#### **The Job Hunt Begins**
Since early January, I've been applying to basically anything i could. At the time of writing this, I've already had rejections from 4/5 employers. Even though it's still super early in the search, it's hard not to get sucked into the negative thoughts of, "it's going to take 6months to a year to even get a call" or "You need to become a senior developer before you can get a call for a junior position". 

While I was in school, I was fortunate to be taught by some amazing instructors, and one of them was able to refer me to a company that was looking for a junior full-stack developer, but more of a focus on the backend. (this felt like it was right in my wheel house, as I had done many projects in school and currently working on others now that are backend focused). I did some research into the company and it seemed like it would be a perfect fit for me as a new grad. So I sent in my application. Within a day, I had a response asking for a phone interview. I was ecstatic, but also very nervous. It had been a long time since I had done an interview, and I was worried that I would be too rusty to do well.

#### **The Interview**
The interview was scheduled for the following day. I spent the entire day before preparing. I went over all the projects I had done in school, and the ones I was currently working on. I also went over some of the questions that I had been asked in previous interviews. I wanted to make sure that I was as prepared as I could be. The interview went well, and I was able to answer all the questions that were asked of me. I was also able to ask some questions of my own, which I think helped to show that I was interested in the position. It was originally scheduled for an hour, and we ended up talking for almost 2 hours! The overall impression I got was amazing, the interviewers made me feel very welcome and comfortable. (If you are reading this, thank you for that!). I went to bed that night feeling very confident that I had done well.

#### **The Day After**
Less than 12 hrs later, I had received a text message and an email to a very short notice 2nd interview. I was about 2 hrs to prepare, which is way less time than I would have liked, but I wasn't going to let that be a reason for me to say no. This sort of situation felt very similar to walking into a final exam you hadn't studied at all for. Admittedly, I think I got in my own head with this one, as this second interview was the technical coding interview. I hadn't really done these questions since school, and I was extremely worried about falling flat on my face. Despite my worries I spent the 2 hrs prepping as much as I could, and when the time came, I dove in head first fully expecting I'd crash and burn.

#### **The Second Interview**
I was greeted by an extremely friendly group during the interview. They explained how the interview was going to go: I was going to be asked 2 technical questions, and then after the fact I'd have the chance to ask them some questions when the coding portion was over. The first question was a simple one. The classic, "remove duplicates from an array". "YES!" I thought. I've seen this before, and conceptually I knew about a Set, which can't contain duplicates. I can pass the input array to the Set, and then return the Set casted as an array. BOOM! 

I was filled with confidence after that. Maybe I do kinda know what I'm doing here... only to immediately have the wind taken out of my sails, the boat capsized with a hole in it and I was eating by the shark that was the "grouped anagrams" question. I never saw this question before, and (you can laugh at this) I didn't know what an anagram was. I was stumped, and thought of a solution - because I wasn't exactly sure how to solve it, I thought I'd go with a brute force solution in hopes to better understand what was happening, knowing that it would be inefficient because it was O(n^3), then after come up with a better solution. 

I was extremely fortunate to have such a great interviewer. I was able to let him know I was stuck on the optimal solution, and he walked me through how to get there. However, during that walkthrough my brain absolutely melted. I swear to god I forgot how to code. I couldn't make an array, I was stumbling on my words. Embarrassed to the max for sure. Simple syntax errors, forgetting how to use a for loop on a dictionary, like what was I thinking?? Eventually we got to the solution, but I'm sure I looked like a complete idiot, and I was flustered for sure. 

Despite all of that, I was happy that we got to the solution at all. I learned a TON about the question and the small errors I was making as well. My biggest hope was that I was able to show that, even though I struggled and was stuck, I wanted to continue to find out more about the question and continue working towards solving it. Personally that was my biggest goal, was showing my enthusiasm to learn. 

#### **The Aftermath**
The coding portion was over, and I felt like an absolute BLOB. I was super embarrassed about my fumble on the second question, especially after learning about the optimal solution (sort the anagrams, then have them as key to your dictionary, then append each of the words to a list in the dictionary). I asked my questions about the company to the interviewer, and then the interview ended. I was left feeling like I had fumbled the ball, and that I had no chance of getting the job. I was extremely disappointed in myself, and I was sure that I had blown my chance.

I won't know of the outcome until monday, but I'm going to stay positive. There's a lot that I learned through this process that I can take into my future interviews. Regardless of what the outcome is, there was growth here and at the end of the day, all I'm trying to do is improve as a developer a little bit each day, and over time that will lead to success.

I will update this post with the outcome of the interview.

Edit: Unfortunately I didn't get the job, however, I was told that I was a great candidate. I'm not going to let this get me down, I'm going to keep pushing forward! I'm greatful for the opportunity to interview with them, and I'm going to take what I learned from this experience and apply it to my future interviews.
